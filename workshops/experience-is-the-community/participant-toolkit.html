The idea behind this workshop

Nobody teaches this. That's the point.

Managing people in an open source community is its own discipline. It isn't taught in schools. It isn't covered in company onboarding. There's no LinkedIn Learning course for "how to make a volunteer feel like their first contribution mattered."

That knowledge lives in people. Specifically, in the people who've done it — who got it wrong in front of everyone and kept going anyway.

"The experience is what makes people stay in communities. And it's also what makes them leave."

Not the tools. Not the platform. Not whether you used Meetup or Lu.ma. The human experience of being inside your community — whether someone felt seen, useful, welcomed — that is the only thing that determines whether they come back.

Open source communities are, accidentally, one of the best free leadership schools in the world. No tuition. No gatekeeping. Just people learning from people, in public, by doing. This workshop is about making that visible — and intentional.

PART 1 — Understand (25 min)

Opening

5 min · Facilitator-led

No slides. No introduction yet. Open with this — and mean it:

"Think of a community you left. Not because life got busy. Because something felt off. Hold that."

Pause. Five full seconds. Then:

"Now think of a community you stayed in way longer than made any practical sense. What was different?"

Don't collect answers. Let people sit with both. Then introduce yourself — one minute, one real story, no CV recitation.

The silence is load-bearing. Don't fill it. The best thing you can do in those five seconds is look calm and wait. People need a moment to actually remember something real.

Mini-talk: The Double-Edged Truth

10 min · Facilitator-led

Conversational. No more than 4 slides if you use any. The core argument in plain language:

  • Nobody teaches community leadership. Not schools, not companies. The knowledge lives in people who've done it.
  • Open source communities are accidentally one of the best free leadership schools in the world — because you can only learn it by being inside one, with other humans.
  • That's also why it's powerful as a draw. The learning requires the belonging.
  • And then the line that anchors everything: the experience is what makes people stay. And it's also what makes them leave.
This should feel like a conversation, not a keynote. If you find yourself reading slides, stop. Put them away.

Activity 1: The Moment

10 min · Table brainstorm
"As a table: brainstorm moments that make someone feel like they belong in a community — and moments that make them quietly decide not to come back."
  • Green stickies for belonging moments. Red stickies for leaving moments.
  • Stick them on the table in two columns.
  • Aim for quantity — 10+ per color per table. Fast and instinctive, not precious.
  • No sharing with the room yet. Just generating.
Why this works People know more than they think they do. This activity surfaces the tacit knowledge that's already in the room before anyone has named a framework. You'll see the same stickies across every table — and that repetition is part of the point.

PART 2 — Design (45 min)

The Experience Map: Framework Intro

10 min · Facilitator-led

Introduce the four zones. Each table has a large printed map with these zones marked.

Zone 01
First Contact
How did they find out you exist?
Zone 02
First Step
What was the first thing they did — and how did it feel?
Zone 03
First Belonging
When did they first feel in, not just attending?
Zone 04
First Contribution
When did they first give something back — and how was it received?
Most communities design zones 1 and 2 accidentally and barely think about 3 and 4.

The drop-off almost always happens in the gap between First Step and First Belonging. That's where people quietly decide whether to return. And almost nobody is designing for it.

Activity 2: Map Your Community

15 min · Individual then table
  • 3 minutes alone first. Individual worksheets. Each person maps their own community against the four zones. This is private thinking time — protect it. No partner talking, no group pressure.
  • Table work (12 min). Place stickies from Activity 1 onto the zones where they belong. Add new ones as they come up. Yellow stickies for anything your community hasn't figured out yet.
  • By the end, each table has a full color-coded Experience Map of a real community — or a composite of several.
Don't skip the individual time. If tables go straight to group mode, the loudest voice dominates and the honest self-reflection never happens. Three minutes of private thinking produces better group conversation.

Gallery Walk

15 min · Room-wide movement
  • Each table posts their map on the wall or flipchart behind them.
  • Everyone gets up and walks the room. 2–3 minutes per table.
  • Participants can add a sticky to another table's map: green if they recognize something that works in their community too, red if they've seen that problem, yellow if it raises a question.
  • No facilitation during this. Movement and reading and adding. That's it.
Your job during the gallery walk is to stay out of the way. Resist the urge to explain tables' maps to visitors. Walk around, look interested, say nothing unless someone directly asks you something.

Gallery Debrief

5 min · Full room

Bring everyone back. Ask three people to share one thing they noticed on someone else's map — something that surprised them, or stuck with them.

Don't over-facilitate. Three shares, then move on. The patterns will be obvious — let the room name them, not you.

If energy dips here, it's usually because the gallery walk ran long. Keep the debrief tight. The point isn't to process everything — it's to name the patterns and move.

PART 3 — Reflect (20 min)

Mini-talk: You Are Part of the Experience

8 min · Facilitator-led

This is the part most facilitators skip. Don't.

The experience of a community is shaped by many things. But more than anyone else, it's shaped by the people leading it. Whether you remember names. Whether you make space for new people or keep gravitating to the same five. Whether someone's first contribution is celebrated or quietly ignored.

"You are not neutral. You are the experience for a lot of people. Especially the new ones."

And this is why burnout is a community problem, not just a personal one. When the leader is running on empty, people feel it before you say a word.

Close with: "None of this requires money or a bigger team. It requires attention. And you already know how to pay attention. That's why you're here."

This talk needs to land personally. Share something real — a moment you got it wrong as a leader. Not a polished failure story. An honest one. The room will feel the difference.

Activity 3: One Thing

7 min · Table then full room
"What is one change — small enough to do next week — that would improve the experience in your community?"
  • Each person writes one idea on a green sticky.
  • Place them in the center of the table.
  • Table picks their single strongest one and writes it large on a fresh sticky.

Closing Wall

5 min · Full room

Every table's "one thing" goes on a shared wall at the front. Facilitator reads them out as they go up. No commentary — just naming them.

Close with:

"The experience is everything. You already knew that. Now you have words for it — and a wall full of proof that you're not alone in figuring it out."
Leave the wall up. Don't take it down after the workshop. Leave it up for the rest of the conference. People photograph it. It travels further than the session itself.

Part 03

Facilitator Guide

Everything you need to run this workshop yourself — including what to say, what to watch for, and how to adapt it for different contexts.

Who can facilitate this

You don't need to be an expert. You need to have lived it.

This workshop was designed to be passed on. You don't need a facilitation certification or a coaching background. What you need is real experience inside an open source community — the kind where you've watched someone quietly stop coming, or seen a first-time contributor light up when their pull request got acknowledged.

If you've felt the difference between a community that has it and one that doesn't — you can facilitate this.

The most important thing you bring A real story. Not a polished "here's what I learned" story. A moment where you got it wrong, or where you saw someone else get it right unexpectedly. That story is what gives permission for the room to be honest.
Before the session

Preparation Checklist

  • Read the full run sheet at least once before the day
  • Prepare your personal story (opening + Part 3 talk) — keep it under 90 seconds each
  • Print Experience Maps at A3 or larger — one per table
  • Print individual worksheets — one per participant
  • Print take-home cards — one per participant, double-sided if possible
  • Prepare sticky notes in three colors — enough that tables don't run out
  • Confirm room is set in clusters, not rows
  • Identify wall space for each table's gallery walk posting
  • Have markers at every table — fat ones, not biros
  • Do a walk-through of the room before participants arrive
  • Set a large visible timer for activities (phone on table, projected clock, or just announce)
What to watch for

Common Moments & How to Handle Them

The table that goes quiet

Some tables will have one person who talks and four who listen. Don't intervene immediately. Give it 90 seconds. If the dynamic isn't shifting, ask the quiet people directly: "What does your community look like in this zone?" — addressed by name if you know them, or just by eye contact.

The person who wants to solve everything

Every workshop has someone who wants to skip diagnosis and go straight to solutions. In Activity 2, that's premature. Redirect: "That's a great solution — hold it for Activity 3. Right now we're just mapping what's true."

The gallery walk that turns into hallway chat

People start talking in pairs and stop moving. This is fine for a minute but kills the energy if it goes on. Give a gentle verbal cue: "Two more minutes — try to see at least one more map you haven't looked at."

Running over time

If you're running late, cut from the Gallery Debrief (take it to 2 min) and the closing mini-talk (cut to 5 min). Never cut Activity 3 or the Closing Wall — those are what people remember and take home.

Someone gets emotional

It happens. Community leadership is often lonely and exhausting work. If someone gets visibly moved during reflection, don't rush past it. Acknowledge it briefly: "This work matters — and the fact that it matters is exactly why it's hard." Then continue. Don't make it a moment, but don't erase it either.

Adapting this workshop

How to Modify for Different Contexts

60-minute version

Cut Activity 1 (The Moment) and go straight to the Experience Map after the opening talk. Shorten gallery walk to 10 minutes. Keep Activity 3 and Closing Wall intact.

Online version

Use Miro or FigJam with pre-built zone templates. Replace gallery walk with a 5-minute async comment round — participants drop stickies on each other's boards before the debrief.

Smaller groups (under 15)

Run as one group instead of tables. Activity 1 becomes a pair share. Gallery walk becomes a single shared map. Works well for leadership teams or co-organizer retreats.

Longer version (2–3 hours)

Add a fifth zone to the map: First Leadership — when did they first step up to lead something? This opens a conversation about succession and the pipeline of future organizers.
