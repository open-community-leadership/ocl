## Why this matters

Most open source communities are one person's burnout away from collapse.

Sustainability is not about preventing the organiser from getting tired. It is about building a community that does not depend on any single person to survive. Succession is the deliberate practice of making yourself replaceable — which is one of the hardest things a community leader can do.

The communities that last are the ones that learned to reproduce themselves.

## What you will learn

- Why single-point-of-failure communities are so common — and so fragile
- How to distribute knowledge before it is too late
- What succession actually looks like in a volunteer community
- How to pass the torch without burning out first

## The knowledge problem

Most community leadership knowledge is undocumented.

It lives in the founder's head: who to contact for venue, how the budget works, which volunteer needs a personal invitation, what went wrong two years ago and why.

When that person leaves — by choice, by burnout, by life — the knowledge leaves with them. The next person starts from scratch. Often, there is no next person.

Documentation is not glamorous. But it is how communities outlive their founders.

---

## The succession conversation

Succession is uncomfortable because it requires admitting that you will not always be there. Many community leaders avoid it — not because they plan to stay forever, but because starting the conversation feels like giving up.

It is the opposite. Starting the succession conversation early is an act of care for the community you built.

---

## Activity: The bus factor audit

Ask this honestly: if you were unavailable for three months, what would happen to your community?

Then identify:

- **What would stop** — things only you know how to do
- **What would struggle** — things others do but without full context
- **What would continue** — things that have genuinely been distributed

The first column is your succession risk. Start there.

---

## Activity: Document one thing

Choose one piece of knowledge that only you hold.

Write it down. Not in your head, not in a private note — in a place someone else can find it.

It does not need to be perfect. It needs to exist.

---

## Activity: Name your successor

Think about who in your community could lead it if you stepped back.

If no one comes to mind immediately, that is important information. It means the Mentorship dimension of POPCOM needs attention now.

If someone does come to mind — have you told them? Have you started preparing them?

---

## Action

Have one succession conversation this month.

It might be with a co-organiser. It might be with a long-term volunteer. It might be with yourself.

Start with: what would you need to know to run this without me?
