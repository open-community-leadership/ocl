**POPCOM dimension: Participation**

---

## Why this matters

Attendance is not participation. Someone can come to every event and never feel like they are part of the community. The gap between showing up and actually participating is where most communities lose people — quietly, with no obvious cause.

Participation is the moment someone moves from observer to member. It is the first time they speak, contribute, help, or take on something small. That moment rarely happens by itself. It usually needs to be designed.

## What you will learn

- The difference between attendance and participation
- What blocks people from participating — and it is rarely shyness
- How to create visible, low-barrier first steps
- How to make participation feel safe rather than performative

---

## The participation barrier

Most participation barriers are structural, not personal.

People do not participate because:

- They do not know what participating looks like in your community
- The first step requires too much — too much knowledge, too much visibility, too much risk
- There is no obvious opening — conversations are already happening, roles are already taken
- They tried once and nothing came of it

The fix is almost never "be more welcoming." It is designing a first step that is small enough to be safe, visible enough to be seen, and meaningful enough to matter.

---

## Activity: Map your participation ladder

Draw a simple ladder with five rungs.

Label the bottom rung: **first contact** — how someone finds out you exist.

Label the top rung: **active participant** — someone who regularly contributes in some way.

Fill in the rungs in between. What are the actual steps from the bottom to the top in your community?

Then ask:

- Where is the biggest gap between rungs?
- What would make that gap smaller?
- Is each step visible to someone who has just arrived?

---

## Action

Design one new first-participation opportunity for your community.

It should be:

- **Small** — takes less than five minutes or requires no preparation
- **Safe** — no risk of embarrassment or judgment
- **Visible** — someone will notice and acknowledge it
- **Meaningful** — it contributes something real, however small

Examples: answer one question in the chat, add your name to a shared document, introduce yourself in one sentence, help carry something, volunteer for one small task.
