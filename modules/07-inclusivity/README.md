## Why this matters

Inclusivity is not a checkbox. It is an ongoing practice of asking who is not in the room — and why.

Open source communities have a documented diversity problem. But the problem is not only about numbers. It is about the experience of people who are already there. Communities can be demographically diverse and still make certain people feel like guests rather than members.

## What you will learn

- The difference between diversity and inclusion
- How communities accidentally exclude people who are technically welcome
- How to audit who has opportunity in your community — and who does not
- Practical changes that do not require a committee or a policy

---

## The invitation problem

Most exclusion in open source communities is not intentional. It is structural.

The talk CFP goes to the people who are already in the network. The volunteer roles go to the people who were already there. The leadership positions go to the people who have always led.

It is not that new people or underrepresented people are unwelcome. It is that the pathways were built for people who already know the way.

Inclusivity means redesigning the pathways.

---

## Activity: The opportunity audit

Look at the last six months of your community activity. For each category, ask who has had this opportunity:

- **Speaking or presenting** — who has presented? Who has not?
- **Volunteer roles** — who has taken on responsibility? Who has not been asked?
- **Recognition** — who has been publicly thanked or highlighted?
- **Decision-making** — who has been in the room when decisions were made?

Now ask: is there a pattern? Are the same people appearing in every column?

---

## Activity: The invisible barrier

Think of one person in your community who participates less than you think they could.

Ask (or imagine):

- What would they need to participate more?
- Is there something about how the community works that makes it harder for them?
- Have they ever been given a direct, specific invitation to take on more?

The invisible barrier is often not hostility. It is the assumption that people who want to step up will step up on their own.

---

## Action

Issue one direct, specific invitation to someone who has not had one.

Not a general call for volunteers. A direct message to a specific person: we think you would be great at this, and we would like to ask you.

Notice what happens.
