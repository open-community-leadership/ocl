## Why this matters

The first ten minutes of someone's experience in your community are disproportionately important. Not because first impressions are everything — but because most people decide whether to return before they have said a word to anyone.

Welcoming is not just being friendly. It is designing for the moment when a stranger becomes a participant. Most communities leave this entirely to chance.

## What you will learn

- Why onboarding is a design problem, not a personality problem
- How to audit what newcomers actually experience (not what you think they experience)
- How to reduce the gap between arriving and belonging
- One practical change you can make this week

---

## The four welcome questions

Ask these about your community:

1. **Does a newcomer know what to do first?** Is there a clear, visible first action — or do they have to figure it out?
2. **Does anyone notice they are new?** Is there a person whose job it is to welcome, or does it happen by accident?
3. **Do they leave knowing at least one name?** Human connection in the first session is the strongest predictor of return.
4. **Do they know how to come back?** Is the next step obvious, or do they have to find it themselves?

## Activity: The Newcomer Walk

Do this alone or with your co-organiser.

Imagine you are attending your community for the first time. Walk through the experience step by step:

- How did you find out the event was happening?
- What did you do when you arrived?
- Who spoke to you first — and what did they say?
- What did you do during the event?
- What happened at the end?
- How did you find out about the next event?

Write down what actually happens — not what should happen.

Then mark each step: **smooth**, **unclear**, or **missing**.

The unclear and missing steps are your onboarding gaps.

---

## Action

Choose one gap from the newcomer walk.

Design one change that makes that step smoother. Test it at your next event.

It does not have to be big. A single person assigned to welcome newcomers, a visible sign, a message in the chat — small changes in the first ten minutes compound over time.
