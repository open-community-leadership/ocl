## Why this matters

Volunteers are not free labour. They are people who chose to give their time because something matters to them. The moment that stops feeling true — the moment they feel used rather than valued — they leave. Usually without saying why.

Recognition is not just thank-you posts at the end of the year. It is the ongoing, visible acknowledgement that someone's contribution matters. It is the difference between a volunteer who stays for years and one who quietly disappears after three events.

## What you will learn

- Why volunteers leave — and it is rarely about time
- How to give ownership, not just tasks
- What recognition actually looks like in practice
- How to build a volunteer culture that sustains itself

## The ownership shift

There is a difference between giving someone a task and giving them ownership.

A task says: do this thing, the way I would do it, and report back.

Ownership says: this is yours. You decide how it works. I trust you.

Most community organisers give tasks when they mean to give ownership. The result is volunteers who feel like assistants rather than co-builders — and eventually stop volunteering.

---

## The recognition problem

Recognition in most communities follows the same pattern: a public post after a conference thanking volunteers by name. That is not nothing. But it is not enough.

Real recognition happens in the moment. It is specific. It names what someone did, not just that they helped.

"Thank you for helping" is acknowledgement. "You completely changed how registration felt for newcomers this year" is recognition.

---

## Activity: Volunteer experience audit

Think of your three most active volunteers. For each one, answer:

- Do they have clear ownership of something — or do they wait to be assigned tasks?
- Have you told them specifically what their contribution has changed?
- Do they know what their path forward looks like, if they want one?
- When did you last ask them how they are doing — not what they are doing?

---

## Activity: The ownership inventory

List every regular task in your community.

Mark each one: **owned** (someone has clear responsibility and autonomy) or **assigned** (someone does it when asked).

Look at the assigned column. Which of those could become owned — if you trusted someone with it fully?

---

## Action

Choose one task that is currently assigned and convert it to owned.

Have a real conversation with the volunteer: this is yours now. You can change how it works. I will support you but not direct you.

Then let them do it their way.
